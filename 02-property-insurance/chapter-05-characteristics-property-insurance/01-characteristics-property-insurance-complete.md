|---------------|---------------|
| **Named Perils** | ONLY covers perils listed on the policy |
| **Broad Form** | Covers ALL losses EXCEPT what's excluded |

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript (48.87s-59.28s):** "So you can think of named perils and broad form as mirror opposites. One insures only the stuff that we list. Broad form insures everything that's not listed."

> **Plain Dumb Down Version (Named Perils vs Broad Form):**
> - **Named Perils**: "We will pay for THESE things: Fire, Lightning, Theft..." If it's not on the list, too bad!
> - **Broad Form**: "We will pay for EVERYTHING... EXCEPT these things: Earthquake, War, Flood..." If it's not on the exclusion list, you're covered!
> 
> Think of Named Perils as a "Short Menu" (only a few options). Broad Form is an "All-You-Can-Eat Buffet MINUS a few items."

## ðŸ”¥ Part 3: Direct Damage vs Indirect Loss

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript (61.24s-81.46s):** "The other characteristic of property insurance policies is that they insure direct damage only. So direct damage or direct loss is one where the insured property is damaged by the peril, for example fire damaging the building. ### Direct vs Indirect:

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Direct Damage** | Property damaged BY the peril | Fire damages the building |
| **Indirect Loss** | Result OF the direct loss | Lost income because store burned down |

> [!TIP]
> **Plain Dumb Down Version (Direct vs Indirect):**
> - **Direct**: The fire TOUCHED your building. The building is burned. That's direct.
> - **Indirect**: The fire burned your store, so you couldn't open for 3 months. You lost $50,000 in sales. That $50,000 is INDIRECT (the fire didn't touch the money, but you lost it because of the fire).
> 
> **Property insurance typically covers DIRECT damage only.** If you want indirect loss coverage (like lost income), you need a separate policy!

### ðŸ“‹ Transcript Example: $500 Deductible

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript (171.13s-194.03s):** "Let's say for example you had a $500 deductible. If you had a $500 deductible, you would not make any claims for losses that are less than $500. |----------|--------|

> [!TIP]
> **Plain Dumb Down Version (Deductible):**
> The deductible is YOUR portion. If you have a $500 deductible and $500 in damage, you get $0 from insurance. If you have $2,000 in damage, you pay the first $500, and insurance pays the other $1,500.
> 
> **Why does it exist?** If everyone claimed every $50 broken window, the insurance company would drown in paperwork and everyone's premiums would skyrocket. The deductible forces you to handle small stuff yourself.

### 2 Guarantees:

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript (207.87s-237.54s):** "The first thing is that it guarantees payment to the mortgagee when the insured is denied coverage due to breach of conditions. | # | Guarantee |
|---|-----------|

| 2 | **No reduction/termination without notifying mortgagee** |

### Why Does the Mortgagee Have Insurable Interest?

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript (238.88s-245.88s):** "The mortgagee of course has insurable interest in the property. The mortgagee is the lender. They provide financing for the purchase of that property."

| Mortgagee |
|-----------|
| Has insurable interest in the property |

> [!TIP]
> **Plain Dumb Down Version (Mortgagee Insurable Interest):**
> The bank gave you $400,000 to buy a house. If that house burns down, they want their $400,000 back. That's their "insurable interest."

> [!TIP]
> **Plain Dumb Down Version (Mortgage Clause):**
> The bank loaned you $500,000 to buy a house. If the house burns down, the bank wants their money protected.
> 
> - **Guarantee 1**: If YOU commit fraud and the insurance company refuses to pay YOU, they still have to pay the BANK. The bank is innocent!
> - **Guarantee 2**: If the insurance company wants to cancel your policy, they MUST tell the bank first. The bank doesn't want to wake up one day and find out the house is uninsured.

> - **ACV = Replacement MINUS depreciation**
> - **Laptop as doorstop = NO replacement cost**

### Key Points to Remember:

| Topic | Remember |
|-------|----------|
| **Personal vs Commercial** | Personal = non-business, Commercial = business |
| **Named Perils** | Only listed perils covered |
| **Broad Form** | All covered except exclusions |
| **Direct Damage** | Property damaged by peril |
| **Indirect Loss** | Result of direct loss (lost income) |
| **Peril Exclusions** | Nuclear, earthquake, wear/tear, war |
| **Property Exclusions** | Auto, aircraft, vacant, money |
| **Deductible** | Higher = cheaper premium |
| **Mortgage Clause** | Bank paid even if insured denied |
| **Warranty** | Breach = policy void |
| **Pairs/Sets** | 1 shoe = $100 not $200 |
| **3 Indemnity Numbers** | ACV, Interest, Limit (smallest wins) |
| **ACV** | Replacement minus depreciation |
| **RC Buildings** | ACV first, then remainder after repair |
| **RC Personal** | Must be used for original purpose |
| **Valued Policy** | Appraisal determines limit |
