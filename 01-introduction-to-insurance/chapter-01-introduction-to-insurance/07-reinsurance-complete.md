### Reason 2ï¸âƒ£: Manage Reserve Ratios (Meet Insurance Act / GAAP Rules)

## ðŸ“‹ 4 Types of Reinsurance Contracts

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "There are four different types of reinsurance contracts and they vary based on the way they're placed and the way premiums and losses are shared."

---

### Type 1: Treaty Reinsurance

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "Treaty reinsurance covers all policies within a particular class. So let's say restaurants - you can arrange a treaty reinsurance policy for restaurants and all restaurants that you write are covered by this treaty reinsurance policy."

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Coverage** | ALL policies within a class/category |
| **Arrangement** | Pre-negotiated, automatic |
| **Example** | "Reinsure ALL our restaurant policies" |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** It's a blanket agreement. Once you set it up, every new policy in that category is automatically covered. No negotiation needed for each individual policy.
>
> **Like:** A bulk discount agreement. "Everything in this category is covered."

---

### Type 2: Facultative Reinsurance

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "This is arranged separately for each policy. So let's say you write a particularly large policy to ensure a skyscraper. You might want to arrange special reinsurance just for that one policy."

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Coverage** | ONE specific policy |
| **Arrangement** | Negotiated individually |
| **Example** | "Reinsure this ONE $100M skyscraper" |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** It's a custom, one-off deal. You negotiate terms specifically for this one big/unusual risk.
>
> **Like:** Ordering a custom suit vs. buying off the rack.

**Quick Comparison:**

| Feature | Treaty | Facultative |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Covers | Whole class | Single policy |
| Automatic? | Yes | No - negotiate each one |
| Best for | Routine business | Large/unusual risks |

---

### Type 3: Proportional Reinsurance

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "All of the business is reinsured and you share all losses and premiums based on a percentage split. So this helps you balance the frequency and severity of losses. So let's say I have a $100 million insurance portfolio. I can arrange a proportional reinsurance policy on a 50-50 split. So when I receive premiums, half of that goes to the reinsurance company and when I pay losses, half of that is paid by the reinsurance company."

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **How It Works** | Share BOTH premiums AND losses by percentage |
| **Split** | e.g., 50/50, 70/30, 60/40 |
| **Purpose** | Balance overall book performance |

**The 50/50 Split Example:**

| Event | Your Share (50%) | Reinsurer Share (50%) |
|-------|------------------|----------------------|
| Premium collected: $1,000 | $500 â†’ You | $500 â†’ Reinsurer |
| Claim paid: $800 | $400 â†’ You pay | $400 â†’ Reinsurer pays |
| Net result | $100 profit | $100 profit |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** You share EVERYTHING proportionally. The good (premiums) and the bad (claims). It's like having a business partner who shares both the income and the expenses.

---

### Type 4: Non-Proportional Reinsurance (MOST COMMON!)

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "This is the most common - basically the reinsurance company would pay losses above a certain dollar value. The retention limit is how much the insurance company is responsible for - it's almost like a deductible for a reinsurance policy. The benefit is it helps limit the severity of loss."

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **How It Works** | Reinsurer pays losses ABOVE a threshold |
| **Retention Limit** | Your "deductible" - what you pay before reinsurance kicks in |
| **Purpose** | CAP your maximum possible loss |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** It's exactly like having a deductible on your car insurance. You pay the first $X, then insurance kicks in for everything else.

**The $3M Wildfire Example (KEY!):**

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "Let's say I'm an insurance company and I enter into a non-proportional reinsurance agreement. I agree to retain up to $3 million for wildfire claims in Ontario. I would pay a fee to the reinsurance company for this coverage over a set period of time, say annually. So that summer if wildfires cost $10 million in losses and my retention limit is $3 million - I'm only required to pay the first $3 million in claims and the reinsurance company would cover the remaining $7 million. So it helps me cap my exposure or cap and limit my risk at $3 million."

| Metric | Amount |
|--------|--------|
| Wildfire claims total | $10 million |
| Your retention limit | $3 million |
| **You pay** | $3 million |
| **Reinsurer pays** | $7 million |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English - Step by Step:**
>
> 1. You agree: "I'll keep the first $3M of wildfire losses" (retention)
> 2. You pay a PREMIUM to the reinsurer for this protection
> 3. Wildfire season hits - $10M in claims
> 4. You pay $3M (your retention)
> 5. Reinsurer pays $7M (everything above your retention)
> 6. Your maximum loss is CAPPED at $3M - no matter how bad it gets!
>
> **Why is this the most common?** Because it specifically protects against CATASTROPHIC losses while letting you keep the routine claims.
