### Loss Frequency

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript (696s-726s):** "So when it comes to loss frequency, we look at the likelihood of an event occurring. So this is related to the law of large numbers, which says that the greater our sample size, the more certainty that we have."

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Loss Frequency** | How OFTEN a loss occurs |
| **Law of Large Numbers** | Bigger sample = more accurate prediction |

### ðŸ“‹ Transcript Example: Coin Flip

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript (708s-726s):** "For example, if you flip a coin twice, you might get two heads and come to the conclusion that when I flip a coin, it's 100% heads. If you flip a coin a million times, you're going to get closer and closer to a 50-50 split between heads and tails. So the larger our sample size, the more accurate our prediction is going to be."

| Sample Size | Result |
|-------------|--------|
| Flip 2 times | Might get 100% heads (inaccurate!) |
| Flip 1 million times | Close to 50/50 (accurate!) |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** This is why insurance companies need LOTS of data. Flip a coin twice and you might think it always lands on heads. Flip it a million times and you'll see it's really 50/50.

### Inverse Relationship (KEY CONCEPT!)

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript (737s-769s):** "Usually, unfortunately, loss frequency and severity have an inverse relationship, meaning losses that are high in frequency are usually low in severity. Whereas losses that are low in frequency are typically high in severity."

| Relationship |
|--------------|
| **High Frequency = Low Severity** |
| **Low Frequency = High Severity** |

### ðŸ“‹ Transcript Example: Shoplifting vs Earthquake

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript (752s-769s):** "A common example would be shoplifting. It's high frequency, but it's low severity. People don't usually steal that much stuff at once. | Event | Frequency | Severity |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **Shoplifting** | High (happens often) | Low (small losses) |
| **Earthquake** | Low (rare) | High (devastating) |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** Things that happen ALL THE TIME (shoplifting) usually aren't that expensive. Things that are RARE (earthquakes) are usually catastrophic.

### Financial Impact Formula

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript (769s-780s):** "Then we want to assess the financial impact of a loss over time, and we would do that by taking the loss frequency and multiply that by the cost of the loss or the loss severity."

| Formula |
|---------|
| **Financial Impact = Frequency Ã— Severity** |

```
EXAMPLE:
Shoplifting:
- Frequency: 100 times/year
- Severity: $50 each
- Impact: 100 Ã— $50 = $5,000/year

Earthquake:
- Frequency: Once every 50 years
- Severity: $1,000,000
- Impact: 1/50 Ã— $1,000,000 = $20,000/year expected cost
```
