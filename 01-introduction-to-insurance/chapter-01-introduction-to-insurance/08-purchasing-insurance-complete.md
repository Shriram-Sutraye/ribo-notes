### Step 4ï¸âƒ£: Get Client Consent (Express or Implied)

**This is required under PIPEDA (Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act)!**

#### Two Types of Consent:

| Type | Description | When Required |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| **Express Consent** | Clear, direct agreement (signature, checkbox) | Sensitive info (credit, health, financial) |
| **Implied Consent** | Assumed based on context | Non-sensitive, obvious uses |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:**
>
> **Express Consent** = "I explicitly agree"
> - Signing a form
> - Checking a box
> - Saying "yes, I consent"
>
> **Implied Consent** = "It's obvious I agree"
> - If you're applying for car insurance, it's implied you consent to them checking your driving record
> - BUT for sensitive stuff (credit checks, health info), you need EXPRESS consent

#### What Makes Consent "Meaningful"?

| Requirement | What It Means |
|-------------|---------------|
| **Clear** | No legal jargon, plain language |
| **Specific** | Exactly what info, exactly why |
| **Informed** | Client understands consequences |
| **Voluntary** | Can withdraw anytime |

#### Sensitive Information (Express Consent Required):

- âœ… Credit reports/scores
- âœ… Health information
- âœ… Financial data
- âœ… Claims history
- âœ… Anything outside "reasonable expectations"

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** If you're going to run a credit check, you CANNOT just assume it's okay. You must:
> 1. TELL the client you're doing it
> 2. Explain WHY you need it
> 3. Get their explicit "yes"

### Q2: What's the difference between express and implied consent?

| Type | How Given | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Express** | Direct, explicit agreement | "Yes, you can check my credit" |
| **Implied** | Assumed from actions/context | Applying for auto insurance implies consent to check driving record |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Rule of thumb:** When in doubt, get EXPRESS consent. It's always safer.

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** The more documentation you have, the better protected you are. Oral is convenient but creates liability.

### Q8: What are loss payees and why do multiple loss payees create moral hazard?

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Loss Payee** | Third party (not the insured) who can collect claim money |
| **Common Examples** | Banks, mortgage companies, finance companies |
| **Moral Hazard** | Increased risk of dishonest behavior |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** If someone has MANY banks/lenders on their policy, it means they have lots of debt. High debt = financial stress = higher chance they might:
> - Commit fraud (arson, staged theft)
> - Neglect property maintenance
> - Let the property fall into disrepair
>
> **Always investigate when you see multiple loss payees!**

### Q9: What's the difference between loss history and claims history?

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Loss** | Something bad happened (damage, accident, fire) |
| **Claim** | You reported it and asked for payment |
| **Key Difference** | You can have a loss WITHOUT claiming it |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** 
> - You had a $500 fender bender but paid out of pocket â†’ That's a LOSS but not a CLAIM
> - When insurers ask about "loss history" they want to know about ALL losses, even ones you didn't claim
>
> **Look-back periods:** Home/Commercial = 3-5 years, Auto = 5-10 years
