## ðŸŒ Lloyd's of London

**The world's largest subscription market for "uninsurable" and complex risks.**

### What Is Lloyd's?

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "Their role is typically to ensure the stuff that is uninsurable in normal markets. Through Lloyd's of London, you can ensure all kinds of stuff - battleships, cruise ships, satellites, they insure all the weird stuff. | Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **What They Insure** | Battleships, cruise ships, satellites, "weird stuff" |
| **What They Are** | NOT an insurance company - a marketplace/group of underwriters |
| **Who Can Join** | Anyone meeting financial requirements |
| **History** | Nearly 200 years supporting Canadian industry |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** Imagine a farmer's market, but instead of selling vegetables, people are selling insurance coverage. >
> **Why "weird stuff"?** Regular insurers don't know how to price a satellite or a cruise ship. Lloyd's has experts who specialize in exactly these unusual risks.

## ðŸ”„ Reinsurance

**Insurance for insurance companies - how insurers protect THEMSELVES from too much risk.**

### What Is Reinsurance?

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "Re-insurance is when one insurer, in this case called the re-insured, cedes the risk to another insurer, the re-insurer. Basically re-insurance is when an insurance company buys insurance on an insurance policy that they wrote or a portfolio of insurance policies that they wrote. What it does is similar to what it does for a regular person - it allows the insurance company to re-insure or pass on or transfer some of the loss exposure to another party."

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Reinsurance** | Insurance for insurance companies |
| **Reinsured** | The original insurance company buying protection |
| **Reinsurer** | The company providing protection to the insurer |
| **Cede** | To transfer risk to a reinsurer |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** Just like YOU buy car insurance to protect yourself from a huge accident bill, insurance companies buy their OWN insurance to protect themselves from huge claims.
>
> **Example:** Intact writes thousands of home insurance policies. If a massive wildfire hits and 500 homes burn down, that's BILLIONS in claims. Reinsurance helps them survive that.

### Why Do Insurance Companies Use Reinsurance?

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "Why is reinsurance used? What are the benefits of reinsurance?"

#### Benefit 1: Increases Capacity

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "It increases capacity, meaning it increases the amount of coverage that the insurance company can take on. Maybe they don't want to have $5 million tied up in one risk. So what they might do is reinsure $4 million of it so it frees up that capacity so they can write other business. Essentially it takes some risk off their books so then they can write more business."

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** Imagine you're a bank that can only lend $10 million total. You lend $5 million to one person - now you only have $5 million left for everyone else. 
>
> With reinsurance: You lend $5 million, then "sell" $4 million of that loan to someone else. Now you have $9 million free to lend again! 
>
> **Same idea:** Reinsurance frees up capacity by taking risk off the books.

#### Benefit 2: Premium Reserve Ratio

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "There are rules that say that they can't write more business than a certain multiple of the cash or liquid assets that they hold. So again they use reinsurance to get some of the money off their books so they can write more business and write more policies. Premium capacity is defined as the amount an insurance company can safely write based on some general accounting principles and also the Insurance Act."

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** There's a RULE: insurers can only write so much business based on how much cash they have. Reinsurance helps them "free up" their capacity to write more.
>
> **It's like a credit limit:** You have a $10,000 limit. If you owe $8,000, you can only spend $2,000 more. Pay off some debt (reinsurance), and now you have more room.

#### Benefit 3: Catastrophe Protection

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "It also offers catastrophe protection from things like natural disasters. Certain catastrophic events can cause insurance companies to pay huge sums of money and potentially go bankrupt. So they use reinsurance or retrocession to spread that risk to prevent a big catastrophic event from taking them out. Similar to how a regular person might use insurance."

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** Wildfires, hurricanes, earthquakes - these can destroy THOUSANDS of homes at once. Without reinsurance, one bad event could bankrupt the insurance company. Reinsurance spreads that catastrophic risk so no single company bears it all.

#### Benefit 4: Exiting a Line of Business

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "They might also use reinsurance when they're leaving a class of business. If you don't want to write a particular class of business anymore, say restaurants for example, and you want to get those liabilities off your books, you can just reinsure your entire portfolio of restaurant insurance policies instead of canceling those customers."

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** Let's say Intact doesn't want to insure restaurants anymore. Instead of canceling thousands of restaurant policies (which would upset customers and agents), they can just REINSURE the whole restaurant portfolio to someone else. Intact keeps the customers happy, but passes the risk to someone else.

### 4 Types of Reinsurance Contracts

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "There are four different types of reinsurance contracts and they vary based on the way they're placed and the way premiums and losses are shared."

#### 1ï¸âƒ£ Treaty Reinsurance

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "Treaty reinsurance covers all policies within a particular class. So let's say restaurants - you can arrange a treaty reinsurance policy for restaurants and all restaurants that you write are covered by this treaty reinsurance policy."

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Covers** | ALL policies within a class |
| **Automatic** | Once arranged, applies to entire category |
| **Example** | "Reinsure ALL our restaurant policies" |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** It's like a blanket agreement. "Anything in this category is automatically covered." No need to negotiate each policy individually.

#### 2ï¸âƒ£ Facultative Reinsurance

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "This is arranged separately for each policy. So let's say you write a particularly large policy to ensure a skyscraper. You might want to arrange special reinsurance just for that one policy."

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Covers** | ONE specific policy |
| **Custom** | Arranged individually for unique/large risks |
| **Example** | "Reinsure THIS specific $100M skyscraper" |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English:** Treaty = bulk deal. Facultative = one-off custom deal for something special.

#### 3ï¸âƒ£ Proportional Reinsurance

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "All of the business is reinsured and you share all losses and premiums based on a percentage split. So this helps you balance the frequency and severity of losses. So let's say I have a $100 million insurance portfolio. I can arrange a proportional reinsurance policy on a 50-50 split. So when I receive premiums, half of that goes to the reinsurance company and when I pay losses, half of that is paid by the reinsurance company."

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **How It Works** | Share BOTH premiums AND losses by percentage |
| **Example** | 50/50 split = you get 50% of premiums, pay 50% of losses |
| **Purpose** | Balance frequency and severity |

> ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English - The 50/50 Example:**
>
> | Event | Your Share (50%) | Reinsurer Share (50%) |
> |-------|-----------------|----------------------|
> | Premium collected: $1,000 | $500 | $500 |
> | Claim paid: $800 | $400 | $400 |
>
> You share everything proportionally - the good (premiums) and the bad (claims).

#### 4ï¸âƒ£ Non-Proportional Reinsurance (Most Common!)

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "This is the most common - basically the reinsurance company would pay losses above a certain dollar value. The retention limit is how much the insurance company is responsible for - it's almost like a deductible for a reinsurance policy. The benefit is it helps limit the severity of loss."

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **How It Works** | Reinsurer only pays losses ABOVE a threshold |
| **Retention Limit** | Amount insurer keeps (like a deductible) |
| **Purpose** | Cap your maximum loss exposure |

**The $3M Wildfire Example (KEY!):**

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript:** "Let's say I'm an insurance company and I enter into a non-proportional reinsurance agreement. I agree to retain up to $3 million for wildfire claims in Ontario. I would pay a fee to the reinsurance company for this coverage over a set period of time, say annually. So that summer if wildfires cost $10 million in losses and my retention limit is $3 million - I'm only required to pay the first $3 million in claims and the reinsurance company would cover the remaining $7 million. > ðŸŽ¯ **Plain English - The Wildfire Example:**
>
> | Scenario | Without Reinsurance | With Non-Proportional Reinsurance |
> |----------|--------------------|------------------------------------|
> | Retention limit | N/A | $3 million |

> | **You pay** | $10 million ðŸ˜° | $3 million ðŸ˜Š |
