### 3 Types of Homeowners Forms:

| Form | Coverage Level |
|------|---------------|
| **Basic** | Named Perils only (13 specific perils) |
| **Broad** | Building = All Risk, Property = Named Perils |
| **Comprehensive** | All Risk for BOTH building and property |

> [!TIP]
> **Plain Dumb Down Version (3 Forms):**
> - **Basic**: The "Cheap" option. Only covers 13 specific things (fire, theft, etc.). If it's not on the list, you're out of luck.
> - **Broad**: The "Middle" option. Your HOUSE gets full coverage, but your STUFF only gets the 13 perils.
> - **Comprehensive**: The "Premium" option. Everything is covered unless specifically excluded. Best protection.

### ðŸ“‹ Transcript Example: Contractor & Homeowner

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript (152.88s-211.12s):** "Let's say you have a house and you hire a contractor to repair your roof or to upgrade your roof. When you hire this person, you are going to request that the contractor add you as an additional insured on their policy. The reason you want to do that is because you want coverage under their policy if they mess something up or hurt somebody while they're repairing your roof. So let's say they're repairing your roof. They drop a hammer and it hits one of the pedestrians in the head that walks past your house. That pedestrian gets injured and so they're going to sue you because you're the homeowner and they're also going to sue the contractor because they were the ones that were primarily liable. So in that scenario, because you are listed as an additional insured under the contractor's policy, the contractor's policy is actually going to protect you, meaning you don't have to use your home insurance or other insurance to defend this claim."

| Scenario | What Happens |
|----------|--------------|
| Hire contractor to fix roof | |
| Contractor drops hammer on pedestrian | |
| Pedestrian sues BOTH you and contractor | |
| You are "Additional Insured" on contractor's policy | Contractor's insurance pays for YOUR defense too! |

> [!TIP]
> **Plain Dumb Down Version (Additional Insured):**
> You hire a roofer. The roofer drops a hammer and hurts someone. That person sues YOU and the roofer. If you're an "Additional Insured" on the roofer's policy, the roofer's insurance company pays to defend you. You don't have to use your own insurance!
> 
> **TLDR**: "Let me ride on YOUR insurance for stuff YOU do."

### Shared Limits

> ðŸ’¡ **From Transcript (274.64s-303.4s):** "Where the additional piece of complexity here is that limits are shared. So if either or both of you create liability together, the limit is still the same. So if you have a million dollar limit, let's say you do something, it's still a million dollars. If somebody else does something, the other named insured does something, it's still a million dollars. If you both act together to create the liability, it's still a million dollars. So it's not a million dollars each. It's a million dollars shared between you two and either one of you can use it."

| Shared Limit Rule |
|------------------|
| Limit is NOT per person |
| Limit is SHARED between all insureds |
| $1M total, not $1M each |

> [!TIP]
> **Plain Dumb Down Version (Shared Limits):**
> Two people on a $1M policy does NOT mean $2M total. It means ONE bucket of $1M that you BOTH share. If you use $600K, there's only $400K left for your partner.
